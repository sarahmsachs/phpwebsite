#dispix: How Disability Looks to Me

About #dispix

On November 11th, 2015, members of PHP1680: "Pathology to Power" presented a photo exhibition about living with a disability. The exhibition featured photo submissions from students and community members, depicting their personal perspectives on life with a disability. #dispix hopes to raise awareness of disability on campus and to amplify the voices and experiences of people with disabilities.

More info on PHP1680: Pathology to Power

PHP1680I: Pathology to Power: Disability, Health and Community offers a comprehensive view of health and community concerns experienced by people with disabilities. Guest speakers, and hands on field research involving interactions with people with disabilities will facilitate the students gaining a multi-layered understanding of the issues faced by people with disabilities and their families.

More Info on Photo Voice

Photovoice seeks to create change. Through photography, individuals offer insight the needs or wants of a community, often a community that has been thus far ignored. Photovoice combines the power of imagery through the personal nature of grassroots social action. The visual impact breaks down barriers by illustrating the struggles of individuals. While Photovoice has now become a large-scale research method, it was first designed in 1992 by Caroline C. Wang (University of Michigan) and Mary Ann Burris (University of London). Together, they designed a method that would allow for silenced voices to be heard. Specifically, the two were working with the women of Yunnan Province in China, who were hoping to improve the area's policies in regards to women. Afterwards, it expanded primarily into the healthcare field, as researchers used Photovoice to demonstrate healthcare inequalities and the concerns of the community.

Photovoice is fundamentally versatile because, unlike many other research methods, it does require extensive resources or skill. The power behind a photo is not the artistry with which it was taken, but rather, the sincerity of the photographer’s experience. Each photo offers a thought to the overall narrative, without pulling attention from those who seek to be heard. When policy-makers refuse to hear the stories individuals have to tell, Photovoice delivers a powerful way to communicate. Photovoice has expanded so that it is used consistently throughout the world to initiate various types of change. Our decision to use photography and thus Photovoice stemmed from its visual nature. As a society, we often define ourselves through pictures, snapshots, and images. As a result, our mental picture of disability is framed through the often limited experiences we may have with disability, and then the rest is filled in through the representation of disability in the media.

Viewing Disability in The Context of Society

Disability is a multi-dimensional experience. Each person living with a disability endures a unique experience. Unfortunately, many people with disabilities hide or are reluctant to talk about their disability due to fear of stigmatization. Disability includes different impairments to any/all parts of the body and can be defined using different models including the medical and social models. Unfortunately, these models begin to create the stigmatization of individuals highlighting their differences and making them believe they must be “fixed” to fit in. In order to fully understand disability, we must remember it does not define the individual, but rather a part of them. The individual has similar aspirations as an “able-bodied” person but must go about achieving them in a different manner. One aspect many people fail to realize is that we all learn from each other’s experiences such that people without disabilities learn from those with disabilities and vice versa. Life would be monotonous and boring if we all had the same physical, intellectual, and emotional abilities as one another.

Disability affects more people than we as a society wish to admit. With a lack of understanding about disability, community members tend to group those with disabilities into a one-dimensional group. This group assumes all have the same opinions, abilities, and experiences. Our project has shown that there are varying degrees of disability ranging from mild to moderate, severe, or profound that illicit unique experiences. Instead of having an outsider assume the meaning of the photo submission, the submitter provided their own caption to eliminate this assumption and provide direct input. The lack of knowledge about disability can also lead into the lack of adequate design of public spaces around the Brown community. Many of the buildings on campus are poor in universal design such that they lack physical access or audio/visual and mental support. The lack of understanding about disability needs to be fixed by more community involvement. Perspectives from people with disability need to stop being pushed aside. They need to be given more credibility as they are the ones experiencing these issues and their input will help fix these concerns.

One of the hardest parts of having an invisible disability like diabetes is that people can't see the thousands of large and small ways in which it affects my life. They don't see the clusters of tiny scars on my fingertips from testing my blood sugar 4+ times a day (I can barely even capture them on camera). Other people do sometimes see my used test strips, because they have a tendency to fall out of my kit everywhere I go (classrooms, the library, my friends' dorms and houses, even my bed). But to other people, it's just one tiny strip. To me, those falling strips are a reminder that I am never without diabetes; it always is and always will be present in my life. Diabetes is only one part of my identity but it affects every part of my life, and these test strips and finger pricks are just a few of the small but significant and constant reminders of my disability.
